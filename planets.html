Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun and the third largest in our solar system, trailing only behind Jupiter and Saturn. It holds the distinction of being the first planet discovered with the aid of a telescope. In 1781, astronomer William Herschel spotted Uranus, initially mistaking it for a comet or star. It wasn’t until two years later, thanks in part to observations by Johann Elert Bode, that Uranus was recognized as a new planet. A day on Uranus lasts about 17 hours—the time it takes the planet to complete one full rotation on its axis. Meanwhile, a year on Uranus, or one full orbit around the Sun, spans approximately 84 Earth years (about 30,687 Earth days). One of Uranus’s most unique features is its extreme axial tilt of 97.77 degrees, meaning its equator is nearly perpendicular to its orbit. This unusual tilt may be the result of a massive collision with an Earth-sized object long ago. Because of this tilt, Uranus experiences the most extreme seasons in the solar system. For nearly a quarter of its year, the Sun shines directly over one of its poles, while the other half of the planet is plunged into a 21-year-long, dark winter. Uranus is orbited by 28 known moons and is surrounded by two sets of rings. The inner system comprises nine narrow, dark grey rings, while the two outer rings include a reddish dusty ring and a blue ring, reminiscent of Saturn’s E ring. Classified as one of the solar system’s two ice giants—alongside Neptune—Uranus’s mass is primarily made up (over 80%) of a hot, dense fluid mixture of “icy” materials such as water, methane, and ammonia.

Cassini–Huygens Mission

  • For more than a decade, NASA’s Cassini spacecraft shared the wonders of Saturn and its family of icy moons.

  • Cassini was a sophisticated robotic spacecraft sent to study Saturn and its complex system of rings and moons in unprecedented detail. It was one of the most ambitious efforts ever mounted in planetary exploration.

  • Discovered icy plumes spraying from fractures on Saturn’s moon Enceladus, leading to the understanding that the moon possesses a global ocean of liquid water, likely with hydrothermal activity on the seafloor.

  • Found Saturn’s rings to be active and dynamic — a laboratory for how planets or moons form.

  • Revealed Titan to have rain, rivers, lakes, and seas; it is shrouded in a thick, nitrogen-rich atmosphere that might be similar to what Earth’s was like long ago.

  • Imaged vertical structures in the rings for the first time; they appear to be made up of particles piled up in bumps or ridges more than 2 miles (3 km) high.

  • In late 2016, the Cassini spacecraft began a daring set of orbits called the Grand Finale, flying just outside its narrow F ring 20 times — essentially a whole new mission.

  • On Sept. 15, 2017, Cassini made its final approach and dived into Saturn’s atmosphere, transmitting data until it burned up like a meteor high above the clouds.

Voyager 1 Mission

  • Voyager 1 is a space probe launched by NASA on September 5, 1977, as part of the Voyager program to study the outer Solar System.

  • The probe made flybys of Jupiter, Saturn, and Saturn's largest moon, Titan.

  • Voyager 1 found that about seven percent of the volume of Saturn's upper atmosphere is helium (compared with 11 percent of Jupiter's atmosphere), while almost all the rest is hydrogen.

  • The Voyagers found aurora-like ultraviolet emissions of hydrogen at mid-latitudes in the atmosphere, and auroras at polar latitudes (above 65 degrees).

  • The lower abundance of helium in the upper atmosphere may imply that the heavier helium is slowly sinking through Saturn's hydrogen; that might explain the excess heat that Saturn radiates over the energy it receives from the Sun.

Voyager 2 Mission

  • Forty years ago, the Voyager 2 spacecraft made its closest approach to Saturn.

  • It returned spectacular photographs of the rings and small shepherd moons such as Prometheus and Pandora that act to herd the rings’ particles.

  • Gathered data on both temperature and pressure. In the highest regions of the atmosphere (at 70 mbar), Voyager 2 recorded a temperature of 82 K (−191.2 °C; −312.1 °F). At 1,200 mbar, the temperature rose to 143 K (−130 °C; −202 °F).

  • The spacecraft also observed that the north pole was approximately 10 °C (18 °F) cooler at 100 mbar than mid-latitudes, a variance potentially attributable to seasonal shifts.

    • Venus is the second planet from the Sun and is often called Earth's "sister planet" due to its similar size and rocky composition. Despite these similarities, Venus has an extremely harsh environment. Its surface temperature averages around 465°C (869°F), making it the hottest planet in the solar system, primarily due to a thick atmosphere composed mostly of carbon dioxide that traps heat through a runaway greenhouse effect. The planet’s atmosphere is also laced with clouds of sulfuric acid and has a surface pressure over 90 times that of Earth’s, equivalent to the pressure found nearly a kilometer underwater on Earth. Venus rotates very slowly and in the opposite direction of most planets, with one rotation taking longer than its orbit around the Sun. The planet has no moons or rings and lacks a magnetic field. Its surface features include vast volcanic plains, highland regions, and large mountains, suggesting significant geological activity. Although Venus’s surface is obscured by dense clouds, radar mapping missions have revealed much about its terrain. Past missions, such as those from the Soviet Venera series and NASA’s Magellan, have helped scientists study the planet, with more advanced missions planned for the future.

    Venera 13

    • Venera 13 returned the first color images of the surface of Venus, revealing an orange-brown flat bedrock surface covered with loose regolith and small flat thin angular rocks.

    • Venera 13 also extended a drilling arm to the surface, picked up a bit of Venusian regolith, or soil, and analyzed it inside a sealed chamber. The results showed that the surface characteristics correspond to compacted ash material such as volcanic tuff [rock]

    • These missions revealed that the soils on Venus are primarily igneous in nature, with a chemical composition similar to basalt and granite found on Earth.

    • Further experiments by Venera 13 and 14 explored the rocks in more detail, hinting at possible sedimentary processes despite the absence of water on the planet. The analysis from these missions suggested that Venus may still be geologically active, as indicated by the presence of sulfur and other volcanic elements

    • It operated under temperatures of ~465°C (869°F) and pressures 90 times greater than Earth’s.

    • Its success was a technological triumph, proving that data collection under such harsh conditions was possible.

    Pioneer Venus Project

    • The Pioneer Venus Project consisted of two separate missions: the Orbiter (launched in 1978) and the Multiprobe.

    • The Orbiter mapped nearly the entire surface of Venus using radar imaging, revealing mountains, volcanoes, and vast plains beneath the thick clouds.

    • The Multiprobe deployed four small probes into Venus’s atmosphere, collecting data on temperature, pressure, winds, and chemical composition as they descended.

    • One of the small probes continued transmitting data for over an hour after reaching the surface, far exceeding expectations.

    • The mission significantly advanced understanding of Venus’s dense atmosphere and cloud structure, including the discovery of lightning-like activity and complex atmospheric circulation.

    • The Orbiter also studied the ionosphere and solar wind interactions with Venus, contributing valuable data for comparative planetology.

    Magellan

    • Magellan was launched by NASA in 1989 and became the first planetary mission to be launched by a space shuttle.

    • It used synthetic aperture radar (SAR) to map 98% of Venus's surface through its dense cloud cover.

    • The mission produced the most detailed maps of Venus ever created, revealing features like vast volcanic plains, large impact craters, and tectonic deformations.

    • Magellan's data showed that about 85% of Venus’s surface is covered with volcanic flows, providing strong evidence for extensive volcanic activity.

    • The mission helped identify coronae—large circular structures possibly formed by mantle plumes—unique to Venusian geology.

    • It concluded in 1994 with a controlled descent into Venus’s atmosphere, gathering data on atmospheric density before disintegration.
